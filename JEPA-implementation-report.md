# Designing a JEPA-Style Model for T-Cell Single-Cell Data

## 1. Conceptual framing  
In a single-cell context, a JEPA (joint embedding predictive architecture) objective would treat parts of a cell’s transcriptome (or other modalities) as **context** and learn to predict the **latent representation** of the withheld part.  For example, one could mask a random subset of genes (or whole gene programs) and train the model to predict the embedding (latent vector) of the masked genes given the unmasked ones.  Alternatively, “context” could be defined as a cell’s expression under baseline conditions and “target” as the latent state after a perturbation (drug treatment or stimulation), or as a neighboring cell state in a pseudo-temporal trajectory.  The best starting point is likely *masked gene prediction*: partition the transcriptome into two sets (e.g. disjoint gene groups or pathways), encode one set and predict the latent of the other. 

This differs from other self-supervised approaches: **masked autoencoders** impute raw expression values for missing genes, whereas a JEPA predicts the *latent embedding* of those genes (so the loss is on the embedding space, not reconstruction).  **Contrastive learning** (e.g. SimCLR-style) instead learns invariance by bringing augmented views of the same cell closer【35†L19-L27】, but does not explicitly predict missing information.  JEPA’s predictive structure is thus more akin to a language-model “fill-in” task at the embedding level than to the pairwise matching of contrastive methods.  Compared to existing single-cell “foundation” models (like scGPT or Geneformer), which often use masked modeling of *raw counts*【67†L102-L110】【67†L209-L217】, JEPA places emphasis on learning a structured latent space.  In practice, one can see JEPA as an intermediate between masked reconstruction and contrastive: it seeks to **decode hidden structure** (e.g. gene–gene interactions or regulatory programs) by forcing the network to predict latent states of withheld data. 

Crucially, a JEPA-style task must be biologically plausible.  Realistic targets might include:  
- **Masked gene modules:** Hide a set of genes (e.g. a pathway) and predict their embedding from the rest.  
- **Perturbation outcomes:** Treat the unperturbed cell as context and predict the latent of the cell under a drug or cytokine stimulus.  
- **Neighboring states:** Consider a cell and its transcriptionally similar neighbor (from pseudotime or paired datasets) and predict one from the other.  
- **Missing modality:** If multi-omics data are available (e.g. RNA + protein), use one modality to predict the latent of the other.  

The **most straightforward first choice** is simple masked-gene prediction in bulk scRNA-seq of T cells: randomly split genes into two groups, encode one group, and train to predict the latent of the held-out group.  This setup naturally extends toward “world-model” goals by iterating masking or by treating perturbations as masks. In contrast, standard masked autoencoders (e.g. scMAE) would reconstruct the expression of masked genes【37†L67-L75】, and contrastive models would train on cell–cell similarity. JEPA’s key idea is instead to **predict hidden structure** (latent embeddings) which may encourage learning predictive relationships (e.g. pathways, regulatory interactions) beyond mere reconstruction or invariance【35†L19-L27】【67†L119-L128】.

## 2. Literature review  
**Self-supervised single-cell models:**  A variety of recent methods transform scRNA-seq data into latent spaces. Classical VAEs like scVI【38†L7-L15】 (Lotfollahi et al.) model counts with a low-dimensional embedding (default 10 dims).  Masked autoencoders (e.g. **scMAE**【37†L127-L136】) corrupt gene values and reconstruct them, similar to vision MAEs.  Many recent foundation models use transformers: e.g. **scGPT** (Cui et al. 2024) and **Geneformer** (Theodoris et al. 2023) are trained on millions of cells with masked token prediction, claiming state-of-the-art in tasks like annotation and perturbation【67†L209-L217】.  In contrast, purely contrastive methods (e.g. CLEAR, CLAIRE) create two views of the same cell (random masking or dropout) and train an InfoNCE loss to bring them together【35†L19-L27】.  For example, SimCLR adapted to single-cell enforces that different “augmentations” of one cell have similar embeddings【35†L19-L27】.  Hybrid approaches also exist (e.g. **scContrast** or **CellLM** use mixed masked reconstruction and contrastive objectives).  

**Multi-omics integration:**  Models like totalVI or MultiVI (VAEs for RNA+ADT/ATAC) fuse modalities but are typically supervised or aligned models, not predictive latents.  JEPA could in principle integrate modalities by treating one as context and predicting the latent of the other.  No JEPA-specific multi-omic work is published yet, but foundation models for multimodal scRNA (like those that accept gene+ATAC tokens) are emerging. 

**Predictive/perturbation models:**  Specialized models aim to predict how cells respond to perturbations.  Pioneers include **scGen** (Lotfollahi et al. 2019) which uses a VAE to model gene knockout effects【62†L67-L69】.  **CPA** (Lotfollahi et al. 2020) and **GEARS** (He et al. 2023) learn to map pre-perturbation to post-perturbation expression.  GEARS in particular embeds each gene and each perturbation and applies a graph neural network on a gene–gene network to predict the outcome【42†L71-L79】【42†L181-L190】.  These models often require seeing each perturbed gene or condition in training, although GEARS can generalize to new gene combinations【42†L71-L79】.  Recent benchmarks, however, show mixed results: deep models often struggle to beat simple baselines.  For example, several studies found that even basic PCA or linear methods match or exceed complex models in predicting unseen perturbations【36†L29-L37】【36†L49-L57】, and a large-scale benchmark reported that pretrained models tend to “compress variance and underestimate synergistic effects” in combinatorial perturbations【52†L85-L92】.  

**Foundation models and limitations:**  Large single-cell models (e.g. scFoundation, scGPT, UCE) boast tens of millions of parameters【9†L6-L14】, but recent reviews caution they rarely outperform simpler methods【67†L119-L128】.  For integration tasks, one study found scVI, CLAIRE (a contrastive model), and fine-tuned scGPT were top performers, whereas zero-shot scGPT failed at batch alignment【38†L94-L102】.  In annotation, studies show simple classifiers often suffice: for example, logistic regression achieved the highest accuracy on cell-type labeling tasks【57†L1-L4】.  Furthermore, unsupervised clustering often fails to segregate biologically distinct T-cell subsets (e.g. CD4 vs CD8 T cells remain mixed by transcriptome)【11†L61-L69】【31†L129-L137】, highlighting that learning T-cell structure is non-trivial.  These findings indicate that any JEPA-style model must be carefully evaluated against strong baselines.  

In summary, major approaches include variational autoencoders (scVI, scANVI), masked AEs (scMAE), contrastive learners (CLEAR, CLAIRE), and large transformer models (scGPT, Geneformer)【67†L102-L110】【67†L209-L217】.  For perturbation prediction, models range from VAEs (scGen) to graph-based simulators (GEARS)【42†L71-L79】.  Multi-omics models (e.g. totalVI) align different data types but are not explicitly predictive.  A gap exists for models that explicitly *predict latent cell states* in a biologically meaningful way.  A JEPA-style approach could fill this gap by embedding predictive tasks into training, but must be benchmarked carefully given that recent foundations sometimes underdeliver【67†L119-L128】【52†L85-L92】. 

## 3. Dataset strategy (T-cell focus)  
A suitable dataset should contain diverse T cells, ideally with perturbations or multimodal measurements.  For a **minimal viable dataset**, one might start with a well-annotated single-cell T-cell atlas. For example, an HCA project profiled ~50,000 human T cells from blood, lung, lymph node and bone marrow (healthy donors, some resting vs stimulated)【27†L23-L30】.  Similarly, Mullan et al. combined 12 10x T-cell datasets (~500K cells) plus a matching CITE-seq panel to form a large “T-cell atlas V2”【11†L61-L69】. These datasets span key T-cell subtypes (CD4, CD8, memory, regulatory, etc.) and can establish context of resting vs activated states. A **minimal pick** could be one of these (e.g. the 50K HCA dataset【27†L23-L30】 or a subset of the 500K atlas【11†L61-L69】) for initial development. It has limited scale but is manageable and already includes known T-cell state annotations.

For a **research-grade extension**, one should incorporate perturbations or multimodality.  Notably, a *primary human CD4+ T-cell perturb-seq* dataset was released, containing 22 million cells perturbed by CRISPRi against essentially all expressed genes【28†L39-L47】. This enormously increases data scale and directly provides perturbation examples (gene knockdowns). Another option is a **pan-cancer T-cell atlas** (~398K cells from 316 patients) that exposes tissue-specific and disease states across cancers【23†L84-L93】. If multi-omics is desired, one could supplement with T-cell CITE-seq or spatial data (e.g. 10x Multiome on immune cells, or smaller CITE panels on T cells), although explicit multimodal T-cell atlases are rarer. 

Key comparisons: pure scRNA vs CITE-seq vs perturbation. A minimal strategy might use only scRNA (easier preprocessing), then extend to CITE-seq (adds protein markers) or ATAC. For perturbations, the CD4 perturb-seq is ideal (large-scale gene perturbations in primary T cells). Two **immune-cell drug-response** datasets were recently announced (likely T-cell cultures under drugs) in a benchmark study【52†L75-L83】 – these would be valuable if accessible. 

In summary, a good plan is: **(1)** start with a moderate atlas (50K–100K T cells, annotated) for prototyping, and **(2)** extend to a large perturbation-focused atlas (hundreds of thousands to millions of cells) for full-scale training. Ensure chosen datasets have good metadata (donor ID, cell type labels, treatment labels) to allow robust splitting and evaluation. All mentioned datasets are publicly available via HCA or institutional repositories. 

## 4. Best-practice model design for initial JEPA  
**Architecture:** A practical first model might use a transformer- or graph-based encoder per modality, plus a latent predictor module. For example, one could embed each gene (or gene subset) as a “token” with a fixed gene embedding (learned or pre-initialized from a network like STRING). If using multi-omics, have separate encoders for RNA vs protein and fuse their latents. GEARS provides one blueprint: it initializes a trainable vector for each gene and for each perturbation, then applies a Graph Neural Network (GNN) over a known gene–gene graph, summing gene and perturbation embeddings to predict outcomes【42†L181-L190】. Similarly, a JEPA model could embed genes and/or cells and use cross-gene attention (transformer) or graph convolutions to propagate information. 

**Latent predictor:** If using a transformer, one can concatenate a special token representing the cell’s global context, or simply mean-pool gene encodings. The latent predictor then tries to map the context embedding to the target embedding (for the masked genes). For example, context genes could be fed into an encoder stack to produce a context vector, and a small predictor network learns to output the target genes’ latent. The loss is on latent similarity (e.g. MSE in latent space) rather than reconstructing counts. To incorporate biological inductive bias, one could use pathway-informed positional encodings (e.g. each gene’s location in a pathway or chromosome) or include a graph adjacency (as in GEARS【42†L181-L190】). 

**Masking strategy:** Random **gene masking** (like BERT for text) is simplest: randomly select a subset of genes (or whole pathways) to hide. Alternatively, one could mask an entire modality (if multi-omics) or an entire cell profile to predict from a paired cell. For context, ensure that the model cannot trivially copy information: mask at least 50% of genes (as vision MAE does).  One can also define context/target splits by pathways or co-expression modules, if prior pathway structure is desired. 

**Biological biases:** Incorporate known batch or donor covariates by conditioning the encoder (as scVI does【38†L19-L27】). For example, add a learned batch embedding vector to the encoder input to prevent learning trivial batch signals. Pathway knowledge can be added either by initializing gene embeddings (e.g. from Gene Ontology) or by adding an extra loss to align latent directions with known programs. In practice, including a simple graph attention mechanism over, say, a protein-protein interaction network can encourage the model to respect gene relationships (as GEARS does【42†L181-L190】). 

**Comparison to other methods:** This JEPA differs from a **masked autoencoder** because it does *not* reconstruct raw gene counts of the masked portion; instead it predicts a latent encoding of them【37†L67-L75】. It also differs from pure contrastive learning (which enforces invariance) because it has an explicit prediction target. Many transformer-based foundations (scGPT, Geneformer) implicitly do masked modeling (with reconstruction loss in count space), whereas JEPA emphasizes latent prediction. Thus, JEPA bridges toward “predictive world models” rather than purely generative or contrastive objectives. 

**Practical tradeoffs:** Transformers (like Geneformer’s 50M-parameter model【9†L6-L14】) can capture long-range gene interactions but are heavy. Graph methods (like GEARS) explicitly use biology but require a quality network and may miss unknown links. Set transformers or simpler attention pools avoid imposing order. For a first implementation by one researcher, a moderate-size transformer (tens of millions of parameters) or a small GNN (a few layers) is realistic【38†L119-L124】. Avoid overly large models or extremely deep stacks initially; instead focus on clear masking and predictor objectives. Use batch normalization and dropout on genes for robustness. Crucially, start with a **simple training objective** (e.g. MSE on predicted vs actual latent) and well-understood augmentations (Gaussian noise or dropout on inputs) to stabilize training【37†L67-L75】. 

In summary, a first JEPA architecture could be: a gene-level encoder (transformer or GNN) taking in masked scRNA counts + batch ID, outputting a context vector; a predictor network mapping that vector to a target latent for the missing genes; and a latent-space loss. Add feature embeddings or graph structure to incorporate pathways if possible. This design avoids reconstructing counts and focuses on the *predictive structure* in the latent representation. 

## 5. Downstream task benchmarking  

### A. Drug perturbation prediction  
**Datasets and splits:**  Use T-cell perturbation data (e.g. CRISPR Perturb-Seq【28†L39-L47】 or new T-cell drug-response sets【52†L75-L83】). Set up tasks such as “leave-one-gene-out” (predict a gene knockout not seen in training) or combinatorial hold-out (predict an unseen pair of perturbations). A robust split is to hold out entire perturbation *conditions* (e.g. all cells with gene X knocked out), ensuring no leakage of that perturbation into training. For drug or cytokine treatments, hold out certain treatments or concentrations. 

**Baselines and metrics:**  Compare against established models: scGen (autoencoder-based)【62†L67-L69】, CPA (Lotfollahi et al.), GEARS【42†L71-L79】 (for combinatorial cases), and transformer-based perturbation models (e.g. PerturbNet). Include simple baselines like principal-component regression (PCA) or linear models, since these have proven strong【36†L29-L37】【36†L49-L57】. Use metrics that capture both overall fit and biological relevance: e.g. Pearson/Spearman correlation or RMSE of predicted vs actual expression, and specialized metrics like “Precision@k” for the top dysregulated genes【43†L1-L9】 or AUPRC on differentially expressed genes. Importantly, report metrics on “delta expression” (change vs control) and recovery of known pathway signatures, not just raw MSE, to assess functional accuracy. 

**Expected performance:**  Given recent benchmarks, a JEPA model should ideally *match or modestly beat* these baselines. Prior work found that even nonlinear deep models often do not greatly outperform simple PCA or linear predictors on perturbation tasks【36†L29-L37】【52†L85-L92】. For example, one benchmark cautioned that foundation models tend to “compress variance and underestimate synergistic effects” on drug combinations【52†L85-L92】. Thus, a realistic aim is to outperform standard methods in specific regimes (e.g. generalizing to new gene combos) rather than indiscriminate gains. If JEPA yields better OOD predictions of novel perturbations, that would be promising. Be mindful that improvements over simple methods (PCA, mean predictor) must be statistically significant and biologically meaningful (e.g. capturing non-additive gene interactions). 

**Key pitfalls:** Avoid data leakage (e.g. the same donor’s cells in train and test) and confirm that the model isn’t just memorizing mean effects. Watch for overly smooth predictions (as noted for pretrained foundations【52†L85-L92】). Also beware of technical artifacts: ensure batch effects or cell-cycle differences aren’t the main signal. Use cross-validation across donors or batches to verify generalization. 

### B. Cell annotation  
**Setup:**  Use annotated T-cell datasets (from blood/tissue) with known subtypes (naïve, effector, memory, regulatory, etc.). The task is to see if JEPA-derived embeddings improve classification of these types beyond marker-based rules. One can train a simple classifier (or even k-NN) on the JEPA embeddings and test on held-out batches. Compare to baselines: classical marker gene gating, logistic regression on selected gene features, or even end-to-end supervised classifiers (e.g. scANVI or supervised transformer). 

**Baselines:**  Traditional annotation often relies on known markers (e.g. CD3, CD4/CD8, FOXP3). A common benchmark is SingleR or logistic regression using a curated panel. Notably, Le et al. (2022) found a simple multinomial logistic regression (with feature selection) outperformed neural nets for cell-type classification【57†L1-L4】. So use a logistic regressor on marker genes as a baseline. Also compare to a fine-tuned foundation model (e.g. scGPT fine-tuned for cell type), or conventional supervised methods. 

**Evaluation:**  Measure accuracy/F1 on held-out cells (from new donors or tissues). However, be cautious: high “accuracy” could reflect label memorization. For example, unsupervised methods often fail for T cells – one study found that standard clustering mixed CD4 and CD8 cells despite clear protein markers【11†L61-L69】【31†L129-L137】. This suggests annotation cannot be solved by clustering alone. We should evaluate whether JEPA embeddings help separate known types better than raw expression. Metrics like F1 per class and confusion matrices are useful. Also consider *biological consistency*: does the model’s notion of a cell type align with known gene programs? 

**Meaningfulness:**  Cell annotation may not fully test a “world model” since it often rewards reproducing known labels. JEPA could potentially outperform marker-based annotation if it captures subtle state differences (e.g. exhaustion states) that single markers miss. However, any improvement must matter biologically (e.g. identifying a rare subset). Given the logistic-regression result【57†L1-L4】, JEPA must significantly beat simple classifiers to justify its complexity. Also use cross-cohort validation: an embedding that works across donors or experiments is more valuable. Finally, consider whether high accuracy on annotation truly indicates a better representation of cellular dynamics – or just better memorization of those categories. 

## 6. Evaluation philosophy and rigor  
Use **robust splits**. For any task, split by donors or batches, not by individual cells, to prevent leakage. In perturbation tasks, hold out entire perturbations or cell types. For annotation, hold out tissues or subjects. Always test *out-of-distribution*: e.g. train on some donors and test on others, or fine-tune on one study and test on another.  

**Baselines:**  Compare to strong, simple methods. As multiple reviews emphasize, foundation models often don’t beat PCA or VAEs【67†L119-L128】【36†L29-L37】, so include PCA-on-residuals, scVI, or logistic regression as controls. Report these side-by-side. 

**Metrics:**  Go beyond aggregate accuracy. For embeddings, report clustering metrics (ARI, silhouette) and biological separability (e.g. how pure clusters are for known markers). For predictions (perturbation or classification), use MSE/RMSE and correlation, but also biologically meaningful metrics: precision/recall of top predicted genes, recovery of differentially expressed genes, overlap with known pathways, etc. Evaluate model uncertainty and calibration if possible. 

**Interpretability:**  Include analyses that check if learned representations make biological sense. For example, compute whether neighbors in latent space share known cell types or functional states more than random. Perform pathway enrichment on high-weight genes for a latent dimension. Check stability by bootstrapping cells or repeating experiments. 

**Common mistakes:**  Avoid inflating performance by mixing data. Do not report only “internal” metrics (e.g. train accuracy) – always show how models generalize. Check that results are not driven by technical confounders. For perturbations, beware of trivial predictions (e.g. predicting no change); ensure metrics penalize this. As Ahlmann-Eltze et al. (2025) and others note, many fancy models provide marginal gains over linear baselines【36†L29-L37】, so claims should be proportionate. Finally, use multiple metrics (e.g. both RMSE and DE gene recovery) since different metrics capture different aspects【52†L85-L92】.  

## 7. Biomarker discovery implications  
In a JEPA representation, “biomarkers” would emerge as the genes most influential to the latent predictions. One approach is to examine feature attributions: for example, compute gradients of a latent dimension (or classifier on top of the latent) with respect to input genes, to rank genes by importance. Genes with high influence could be candidate biomarkers. Alternatively, if the model includes a decoder or a proxy objective on raw expression, inspect the decoder weights for each latent factor. 

Another strategy is to link latent dimensions to known gene sets or pathways. For instance, one might correlate each latent axis with curated gene programs: if latent dim *k* is strongly associated with a T-cell activation signature, then top contributing genes in that program are biomarkers. This is akin to methods like **VEGA** that build latent space from gene modules for interpretability【60†L25-L33】. In practice, one could project cells onto known gene programs (e.g. NMF-derived programs) and see if those align with latent clusters or gradients【31†L159-L168】. 

**Stability:**  Reliable biomarkers should be consistent across donors and cohorts. Test if the same top genes (or pathways) appear important when training the model on different subsets. Sparse or regularized models may yield more stable gene sets. Also check if predicted biomarker genes replicate known biology (e.g. established T-cell markers), which increases confidence in the model’s interpretability. 

**Causality vs correlation:**  Note that JEPA learns correlational structure: genes that the model finds predictive of a condition are not necessarily causal drivers. Many “biomarkers” will be markers of state rather than effectors. Causal inference would require experimental interventions. So biomarkers from JEPA should be seen as strong correlates or hypotheses, to be validated independently. 

In summary, one could extract biomarkers by analyzing the learned embeddings (e.g. via gradient attribution) or by relating latents to gene sets. Constraining the model to incorporate known pathways (as in VEGA)【60†L25-L33】 could make biomarker interpretation more direct. However, one should remain critical: complex models often trade interpretability for predictive power, so it’s possible JEPA improves prediction without yielding clear, compact biomarkers. 

## 8. Practical roadmap  
**Minimal 2–3 month project:**  *Phase 1 (1 month)* – Data & simple model. Acquire a small T-cell scRNA-seq set (e.g. 10–50K cells). Preprocess (normalization, feature selection to ~2–5K genes). Implement a basic JEPA: for instance, a transformer with ~1–2 layers and ~64 hidden units, masking 50% of genes randomly. Use PyTorch or TensorFlow (scvi-tools or HuggingFace Transformers can accelerate development). Train on a single GPU (e.g. RTX 3090 or A100) for a few epochs to ensure convergence. Evaluate by measuring latent reconstruction error and simple downstream (cluster purity, a few held-out genes).  

*Phase 2 (1–2 months)* – Scaling and benchmarking. Scale up to a larger dataset (e.g. 100K+ T cells, possibly multiple donors). Incorporate batch-correction (e.g. batch-conditional encoder as in scVI). Add one downstream task (e.g. classify CD4 vs CD8, or predict knock-out of 1–2 genes). Use held-out evaluation. Compare against baselines (scVI, PCA+RF, etc.). Refine model if needed (add second transformer layer, adjust masking ratio, etc.). 

A *publishable intermediate milestone* could be a workshop paper or preprint showing that a JEPA-style model is feasible and outperforms a basic autoencoder in a toy perturbation prediction. 

**Longer-term roadmap:**  Once the basic model works, extend to multi-omic T-cell data or to joint cell–cell prediction tasks. Move to the larger perturb-seq dataset (22M cells) for full training, which may require multiple GPUs or TPUs. Investigate architectural enhancements (deeper transformer, integrated GO info like scLong) and more sophisticated objectives (predict multiple future states). Eventually aim to approximate a “world model” by iteratively chaining predictions (e.g. multiple masking cycles, or modeling dynamic responses to sequences of stimuli). 

**Compute and libraries:** Use GPUs with ample memory (≥16–32 GB); initially one GPU suffices, later expand to multiple if training on millions of cells. Leverage libraries like scvi-tools (for data handling and VAE baselines) and HuggingFace/Timm for transformer scaffolding. For graph components, PyTorch Geometric could be used if incorporating networks. Expect a first model (2 transformer layers, ~10M parameters) to train in days on a single GPU. 

**Failure modes:** Watch for collapse (the model always predicts the same latent) – monitor training to ensure loss decreases. If too many dimensions or too few data, the model may overfit; use dropout or early stopping. If batch effects dominate, ensure conditioning on batch or include batch-mix augmentation. If performance plateaus below baseline, consider that the JEPA objective may not be well-aligned – try tuning masking fraction or model capacity. 

In sum, start simple and iterate: choose modest model size and a controlled dataset to validate the JEPA concept, then gradually incorporate more complexity (multi-modal inputs, larger scale) only as performance and resources allow【38†L119-L124】【34†L119-L128】. With careful benchmarking and incremental goals, this roadmap balances feasibility and ambition toward a predictive “world model” of T-cell biology.
